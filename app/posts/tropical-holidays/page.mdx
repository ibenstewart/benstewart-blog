# Tropical Holidays

I've taken three proper tropical holidays in the last five years. Beach. Ocean. Palm trees. The whole setup.

Each time, I told myself I'd disconnect. Each time, I spent the first two days checking Slack on a sun lounger.

The third time, something clicked.

---

## The Problem With Disconnecting

Disconnecting sounds simple. Turn off notifications. Don't check email. Be present.

But nobody tells you what to *do* with the space that creates. When you're not filling time with work, you're left with... what, exactly? Staring at waves? Reading a book you've been meaning to read for two years? Actually talking to the people you came with?

<KeyPoint>
The hard part of disconnecting isn't the logistics. It's tolerating the quiet. Most people can't. That's why the poolside bar exists.
</KeyPoint>

Tropical holidays expose this. You can't distract yourself with errands or chores. There's no "productive downtime." It's just you, heat, and time.

Either you rest, or you fill the void with something else.

---

## The Instagram Problem

There's a specific type of tropical holiday where the point isn't rest. It's evidence.

You've seen it. The carefully staged photos. The sunset shots. The "candid" moments that took twelve takes. The post-holiday carousel with the caption about "switching off."

I'm not judging. I've done it. But here's the thing: if you're optimizing for content, you're not optimizing for rest. Those are different objectives. You can't do both well at the same time.

<Callout type="insight">
The test is simple: if your phone died on day one and you couldn't charge it, would the holiday still be worth it? If the answer is no, you're not there for the beach. You're there for the feed.
</Callout>

---

## What Actually Works

The third tropical holiday, I made one change. I left my phone in the room safe for six hours a day. Not because I'm disciplined. Because I physically couldn't get to it without effort.

That's it. No grand philosophy. Just friction.

And here's what happened: I got bored. Properly, deeply bored. For about ninety minutes on day two.

Then I started noticing things. The sound of the waves isn't constant—it changes with the tide. The birds start at sunrise and stop at noon. The couple three loungers over are having the same argument they had yesterday.

By day four, I was reading two books at once, having actual conversations, and sleeping through the night for the first time in months.

<KeyPoint>
Rest isn't something you achieve. It's something you allow. Most of us spend our lives preventing it.
</KeyPoint>

---

## The Return

The real test of a tropical holiday isn't how you feel on the beach. It's how you feel two weeks after you're back.

If you sprint back into the same pace, the same stress, the same inbox zero obsession... what was the point? You just geographically relocated your burnout for a week.

But if you come back with even one habit changed—sleeping better, checking email less, saying no to things—you actually rested. The holiday worked.

I don't know if tropical holidays are uniquely good at this. Maybe it's just forced simplicity. Ocean, sun, sand. Not much else to optimize.

Or maybe it's just that when you're somewhere beautiful and expensive, you feel obligated to actually be there. Can't waste it scrolling.

<TLDR>
Everyone says they want to disconnect on holiday. Few actually do it. Tropical holidays force the question: are you there to rest, or to prove you went? Rest isn't something you achieve through discipline. It's something you allow by removing options. The real test isn't how you feel on the beach. It's whether anything changes when you get back.
</TLDR>
